3.What do you think is the reason for this issue for the supply demand gap?

One reason could be, as explained in the introduction video of the case that in the morning time (assuming airport to be far from city), average waiting time at the airport is more which is not profitable to the driver. Demand at the airport is lesser at the morning time due to less number of incoming flights.

 

4.What is your recommendation to Uber?

Surge pricing (keeping company's margin unchanged) can be one way to deal with this issue. Drivers will be lured to complete the rides to increase/maintain their profits and they won’t cancel.

Another way could be to offer some ‘city-to-airport’ targets per day, given an increased share of takings for all subsequent rides for the day. For example, if a driver gets 75% share from one ride and 25% goes to Uber, if a driver completes say 2 city-to-airport rides in a day, all his subsequent rides for that day will have 80% driver share and 20% to Uber. This way the cancelled trips can be reduced without surge pricing.

Results show considerable gap between the supply and demand of cabs at the airport. This means there are not enough cabs to suffice the demand at the airport in the evening rush hours. One reason could be the peak hour traffic throughout the city at this time slot which delays the average time of journey of the already taken cabs to reach their destinations and then coming back to the airport. In simple terms it means that most cabs are already occupied for most of the time during this time slot. So there are no free cabs available at the airport.

4.What is your recommendation to Uber?

Either the supply can be increased or the existing supply can be optimized to handle the current demand. Supply can be increased by surge pricing (keeping company's margin unchanged) which in turn will bring in more drivers and hence, more cabs. Second option could be the 'Shared Cab' at the airport where more than one requests can be handled by a single cab at the same time.
